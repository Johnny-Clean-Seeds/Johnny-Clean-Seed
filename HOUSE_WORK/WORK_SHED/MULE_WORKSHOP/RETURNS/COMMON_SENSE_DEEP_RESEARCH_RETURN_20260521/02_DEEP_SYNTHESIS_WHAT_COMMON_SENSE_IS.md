# Deep Synthesis - What Really Defines Common Sense

Date: 2026-05-21
Status: REVISED SYNTHESIS / SOURCE MATERIAL ONLY

## Short Definition

Common sense is a layered, situated, defeasible fit system.

It is the capacity and background inventory by which a human quickly forms workable judgments about ordinary reality, other agents, language, norms, risk, and action under limited time, limited attention, and incomplete proof.

## Full Definition

Common sense is not one faculty, one database, one feeling, one logic, one culture, or one moral code.

It is an operating stack made from:

- integrated perception;
- body-world action knowledge;
- early core knowledge;
- intuitive physics;
- intuitive biology;
- intuitive psychology / theory of mind;
- semantic memory;
- categories and prototypes;
- schemas, scripts, and plans;
- learned causal expectations;
- social norms and cultural assumptions;
- common ground in conversation;
- pragmatic relevance control;
- heuristics and satisficing;
- risk-sensitive proof thresholds;
- feedback and correction;
- defeat switches that say when the default no longer fits.

The word "common" does not mean "owned identically by all humans." It means some mixture of:

- shared by ordinary human minds;
- shared by a community;
- shared by participants in a situation;
- ordinary rather than specialized;
- available before formal proof;
- presumed unless defeated.

The word "sense" does not mean only raw sensation. In modern use it means perception, judgment, fit, practical understanding, and meaningful orientation.

## Where Common Sense Comes From

### 1. Biological And Evolutionary Preparedness

EVIDENCE:
Core knowledge research argues that infants and other animals show early structured expectations about objects, agents, quantities, and places.

INFERENCE:
Humans are not blank slates for common sense. Some foundations are early-emerging because organisms need workable expectations before formal teaching.

Examples:

- objects continue to exist;
- objects usually move continuously;
- unsupported things fall;
- agents pursue goals;
- larger amounts differ from smaller amounts;
- other beings can see, attend, want, and act.

Caveat:
Early core knowledge is not adult common sense. It is the root layer, not the whole tree.

### 2. Body-World Coupling

EVIDENCE:
Semantic memory and embodied cognition sources treat world knowledge as connected to perceptual, sensorimotor, contextual, and action information.

INFERENCE:
Common sense is learned by having a body in a world. A person learns what doors, stairs, knives, cups, weather, surfaces, sounds, distance, weight, and other bodies afford.

Examples:

- a shirt is not salad;
- a knife cuts the carrot, not the other way around;
- a full cup spills if tilted;
- a child in arms is probably smaller and younger than the adult holding him;
- a blocked path requires going around or moving the obstacle.

### 3. Developmental Learning

EVIDENCE:
Theory-of-mind research shows children develop the ability to represent others' false beliefs, with classic verbal false-belief task success usually appearing around age 4.

INFERENCE:
Common sense grows. A toddler, child, adolescent, and adult do not have identical common sense. Common sense includes learned models of minds, reasons, rules, danger, trust, and deception.

Examples:

- knowing that another person can be wrong;
- knowing that someone acts from what they believe, not only from what is true;
- knowing that an apology, threat, joke, lie, or promise changes the social situation.

### 4. Semantic Memory And Concepts

EVIDENCE:
Semantic memory is the system of knowledge about the world, concepts, and symbols. Modern reviews say it is flexible, context-sensitive, grounded, and compositional.

INFERENCE:
Common sense requires a large reservoir of ordinary concepts and relations.

Examples:

- lemons are sour;
- dogs are animals;
- glass breaks;
- doctors treat patients;
- restaurants serve food;
- keys open locks;
- money can be exchanged for goods;
- a bruise can signal injury;
- a promise creates an expectation.

### 5. Schemas, Scripts, And Plans

EVIDENCE:
Schema research says prior knowledge shapes memory and learning. Schank and Abelson's script theory shows how typical event patterns simplify inference.

INFERENCE:
Common sense is not only facts. It is event structure.

Examples:

- restaurant script: enter, sit, order, eat, pay, leave;
- doctor's visit script: check in, wait, describe symptoms, examination, advice;
- apology script: wrongdoing, recognition, regret, repair;
- shopping script: choose item, check price, pay, receive item.

Why this matters:
Scripts let a person infer missing pieces. If someone says "I left the restaurant without paying," common sense knows a normal step was skipped.

### 6. Socialization And Culture

EVIDENCE:
Ethnomethodology studies the ordinary methods people use to produce recognizable social order. Social-construction sources treat everyday knowledge as built and maintained through language, institutions, and interaction.

INFERENCE:
Common sense is partly local. It includes what a community treats as normal, polite, dangerous, shameful, obvious, legal, sacred, funny, rude, or practical.

Examples:

- how close to stand to someone;
- whether eye contact is respectful or aggressive;
- when to interrupt;
- what counts as waiting your turn;
- what a handshake, nod, bow, receipt, signature, or silence means;
- what a workplace treats as proof.

Caveat:
This layer is powerful but not automatically true. A group's common sense can encode prejudice, error, bad incentives, stale tradition, or local blind spots.

### 7. Language And Common Ground

EVIDENCE:
Pragmatics treats common ground as information presumed shared by speakers. Clark and Brennan describe grounding as the process by which participants establish that something has been understood well enough to proceed.

INFERENCE:
Common sense includes knowing what is mutually assumed in a conversation.

Examples:

- pronouns need shared referents;
- a command changes obligations;
- a question creates an answer slot;
- "the usual place" only works if both parties know it;
- "do you have the time?" usually asks for the time, not whether you possess time.

### 8. Heuristics And Bounded Rationality

EVIDENCE:
Bounded rationality explains judgment under limited information and computation. Ecological rationality says simple heuristics can work well when matched to the environment. Heuristics-and-biases research shows the same shortcuts can also mislead.

INFERENCE:
Common sense works because humans cannot calculate everything. It is often "good enough for this ordinary case," not "logically complete."

Examples:

- choose the first safe-enough option;
- trust a familiar route unless conditions changed;
- avoid a smoking wire;
- ask the person who probably knows;
- do not optimize a tiny decision;
- escalate when risk is high.

### 9. Feedback Calibration

EVIDENCE:
Many source streams converge on revision: development changes through prediction errors, schemas update learning, conversation grounds understanding, and practical judgment improves with feedback.

INFERENCE:
Common sense gets better when defaults are tested against reality. Without feedback, it becomes stale confidence.

Examples:

- a novice's "common sense" about a machine may fail until real use teaches exceptions;
- a culture's old norm may break under new technology;
- a person's confident hunch may need correction after a bad prediction.

### 10. Defeat Switches

INFERENCE:
A mature common-sense model must include conditions under which common sense should stop being trusted.

Defeat conditions:

- high stakes;
- unfamiliar domain;
- conflicting evidence;
- expert domain with counterintuitive truth;
- cultural mismatch;
- adversarial manipulation;
- rare edge case;
- measurement contradicts the default;
- ordinary appearance is misleading;
- memory is weak;
- incentives distort perception.

Examples:

- "it looks clean" is not proof of sterilization;
- "everyone knows" is not proof in law, science, medicine, or engineering;
- "it feels obvious" can be hindsight bias;
- "that is how we always do it" can hide institutional failure.

## What Common Sense Is Composed Of In Full Working Taxonomy

### Layer 1 - Sensory Binding

The ability to integrate inputs into a single world:

- sight, sound, touch, smell, taste;
- motion;
- object boundaries;
- location;
- continuity.

This is the oldest meaning of common sense.

### Layer 2 - Embodied Affordance Sense

Knowledge of what actions fit what things:

- sit on chairs;
- open doors;
- carry light objects;
- ask help for heavy objects;
- do not touch fire;
- use containers for liquids.

### Layer 3 - Physical World Defaults

Ordinary physics:

- solidity;
- gravity;
- contact causality;
- containment;
- support;
- collision;
- breaking;
- fluid flow;
- heat and cold;
- tool use.

### Layer 4 - Biological Defaults

Ordinary living-world expectations:

- animals move and need food;
- plants grow;
- wounds need care;
- illness can spread;
- dead things do not act;
- babies need protection;
- pain signals damage or distress.

### Layer 5 - Agent And Mind Defaults

Ordinary psychology:

- people have goals;
- people can be mistaken;
- people can lie;
- people can forget;
- attention matters;
- belief guides action;
- emotion changes behavior;
- incentives shape choices.

### Layer 6 - Social Norm Defaults

Ordinary social order:

- turn-taking;
- reciprocity;
- apology;
- promise;
- ownership;
- permission;
- privacy;
- authority;
- fairness;
- reputation;
- blame and repair.

### Layer 7 - Language Pragmatics

Ordinary communication:

- shared reference;
- context;
- implicature;
- relevance;
- common ground;
- grounding;
- speech acts;
- what is meant vs what is literally said.

### Layer 8 - Event Scripts

Routine sequence knowledge:

- meals;
- travel;
- school;
- work;
- shopping;
- meetings;
- repair;
- emergency;
- negotiation;
- conflict.

### Layer 9 - Semantic Network

World concepts and relations:

- categories;
- properties;
- typical functions;
- part-whole relations;
- cause-effect links;
- taxonomies;
- associations;
- analogies.

### Layer 10 - Heuristic Control

Fast decision procedures:

- recognize;
- stop at good enough;
- use default unless defeated;
- ask when uncertain;
- avoid obvious harm;
- spend proof in proportion to risk;
- choose simpler repair first;
- test before scaling.

### Layer 11 - Cultural Doxa

Local taken-for-granted assumptions:

- "normal" roles;
- local etiquette;
- institutional expectations;
- class markers;
- gender/race/status assumptions;
- national habits;
- workplace rules;
- religious/moral backgrounds.

This layer is common sense socially, but it is also where common sense can carry hidden bias.

### Layer 12 - Metacognitive Fit Check

The ability to ask:

- Does this default fit here?
- What could disprove it?
- Is this my culture talking?
- Is this a specialist domain?
- Is there a risk level that requires proof?
- Is this common sense or just familiarity?

This layer separates mature common sense from mere habit.

## How We Know

We know common sense exists and has these layers by convergence, not by one proof.

### Proof Channel 1 - Linguistic History

The word history shows the phrase changed over time from perceptual integration to ordinary practical judgment.

What it proves:
The term is historically layered.

What it does not prove:
It does not prove the cognitive mechanism.

### Proof Channel 2 - Philosophy

Reid and Moore show that common sense can function as first-principle judgment or burden-of-proof pressure against theories that deny ordinary certainties.

What it proves:
Philosophers have treated some ordinary judgments as epistemically important.

What it does not prove:
It does not prove every common belief is true.

### Proof Channel 3 - Infant And Child Development

Core knowledge and theory-of-mind research show early and developing expectations about objects, numbers, agents, and beliefs.

What it proves:
Some common-sense foundations are early and structured.

What it does not prove:
It does not prove adult common sense is innate or uniform.

### Proof Channel 4 - Cognitive Psychology

Semantic memory, schemas, scripts, plans, and heuristics explain ordinary inference, expectation, and bounded judgment.

What it proves:
Common sense depends on organized memory and quick inference.

What it does not prove:
It does not make common sense infallible.

### Proof Channel 5 - Social Science

Ethnomethodology and social construction show everyday order is actively produced through ordinary methods, shared language, and local practices.

What it proves:
Common sense is partly social and cultural.

What it does not prove:
It does not reduce all common sense to culture.

### Proof Channel 6 - Linguistics And Pragmatics

Common ground and grounding show ordinary conversation depends on shared assumptions and active coordination.

What it proves:
Common sense is required for communication beyond literal words.

What it does not prove:
It does not prove all participants share the same background.

### Proof Channel 7 - AI Failures And Benchmarks

AI has long struggled with everyday inference. Commonsense knowledge bases, Winograd schemas, and modern LLM benchmark debates show that common sense is broad, implicit, defeasible, and hard to test.

What it proves:
Common sense is not just grammar, not just data, and not just formal logic.

What it does not prove:
Benchmark success does not prove complete common sense.

## Strongest Practical Definition For Mr.Kleen

For Mr.Kleen work, the best candidate definition is:

Common sense is relation-aware relevance and fit judgment under proof and feedback.

Expanded:

It asks:

- What situation is this?
- What ordinary defaults apply?
- What source or rule matters?
- What scale fits?
- What is likely enough to act on?
- What needs proof before trust?
- What would defeat the default?
- What action is safe, small, and useful now?
- What feedback will correct the model?

Why this fits the house:

- It preserves proof.
- It avoids making "common sense" a king.
- It prevents overbuilding.
- It protects against stale assumptions.
- It connects to MTER, HNS, link maps, source-to-claim maps, rejection ledgers, and tiny tests.

## Final Synthesis Sentence

Common sense is the human ordinary-world fit engine: a learned and partly innate stack of perceptual, physical, social, linguistic, cultural, and heuristic expectations that lets a person act sensibly before full proof, while mature common sense also knows when those defaults must yield to evidence, expertise, risk, or context.
