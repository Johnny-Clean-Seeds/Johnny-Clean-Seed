## Raw Capture

### A. Word History And Old Meaning

EVIDENCE:
Etymonline traces "common sense" to a late 14th century meaning: an inner faculty that unifies the impressions of the five senses, from Latin `sensus communis` and Greek `koine aisthesis`. Later English meanings include ordinary understanding and good sense.

EVIDENCE:
A scholarly source on Aristotle says Aristotle's "common sense" is not the same as the modern English phrase. In Aristotle, it is a perceptual ability, whereas modern common sense goes beyond perception.

INFERENCE:
The term carries a split from the beginning: one line means sensory integration; another line means ordinary practical judgment.

### B. Philosophical Common Sense

EVIDENCE:
The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Thomas Reid says Reid's common sense refers to a select set of intuitive judgments or first principles. It also warns that Reid's role as a "common sense philosopher" is easy to overestimate or misunderstand.

EVIDENCE:
The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on G. E. Moore says Moore used common-sense convictions as a presumption against philosophical theses that deny ordinary things, but Moore's common sense was not a full system like Reid's.

INFERENCE:
Philosophical common sense is often a burden-of-proof tool: if a theory denies ordinary certainty, the theory needs strong reasons. This is not the same as "whatever feels obvious is true."

### C. Developmental Core Knowledge

EVIDENCE:
Carey and Spelke argue that science and later conceptual learning build on domain-specific systems of core knowledge.

EVIDENCE:
Spelke's infant cognition work treats object knowledge as an early, abstract, commonsense understanding of the physical world.

EVIDENCE:
Violation-of-expectation research reports infants are sensitive to surprising events involving objects, number, social agents, and emotions.

INFERENCE:
Some common-sense ingredients begin early: object continuity, contact, containment, number sense, agency, and place. These are not complete adult common sense, but they are likely foundations.

### D. Theory Of Mind And Folk Psychology

EVIDENCE:
The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy describes theory of mind and false-belief tasks. Around age 4, many children pass verbal false-belief tasks and show an understanding that another agent can act from a belief that is false.

EVIDENCE:
The SEP folk psychology entry frames folk psychology as our familiar way of making sense of action using beliefs, desires, emotions, intentions, and related states.

INFERENCE:
Common sense includes an agent model. Humans predict and explain others by modeling goals, beliefs, intentions, constraints, emotions, and attention.

### E. Semantic Memory, Schemas, Scripts, And Plans

EVIDENCE:
A PubMed semantic memory review describes semantic memory as knowledge about the world, concepts, and symbols, and emphasizes modern views that are fluid, context-sensitive, grounded, and compositional.

EVIDENCE:
Nature Reviews Neuroscience on schemas notes Bartlett's early demonstration that memory is reconstructed and influenced by expectations and social norms. It also connects schemas with learning and memory systems.

EVIDENCE:
Schank and Abelson's scripts/plans work uses scripts to simplify inference and plans to make sense of seemingly disconnected sentences and infer intentions.

INFERENCE:
Common sense depends on stored, organized world knowledge. It is not only raw facts; it includes event patterns, roles, typical sequences, preconditions, goals, and likely next steps.

### F. Bounded Rationality And Heuristics

EVIDENCE:
The SEP bounded rationality entry describes Simon's question: how humans solve uncertain problems with limited resources. It describes satisficing as choosing an option that meets a good-enough threshold.

EVIDENCE:
Todd and Gigerenzer describe ecological rationality as domain-specific simple heuristics that can be accurate by exploiting environmental structure.

EVIDENCE:
Tversky and Kahneman's 1974 Science paper is a core source for judgment under uncertainty, showing that heuristics can produce both useful shortcuts and systematic biases.

INFERENCE:
Common sense is not perfect logic. It is bounded, fast, cheap, and environment-sensitive. That makes it powerful in normal cases and dangerous in mismatched cases.

### G. Common Ground And Pragmatics

EVIDENCE:
The SEP common-ground entry defines common ground as information presumed shared by parties to a discourse and shows that utterances depend on and change it.

EVIDENCE:
Clark and Brennan's grounding paper says participants in conversation try to establish that what has been said has been understood well enough to become common ground.

INFERENCE:
Common sense includes conversational sense: knowing what can be left unsaid, what a pronoun refers to, what commitment was made, what context matters, and when mutual understanding is sufficient.

### H. Social And Cultural Production

EVIDENCE:
Ethnomethodology studies the ordinary methods people use to produce recognizable everyday social order, making use of common-sense knowledge in mundane situations.

EVIDENCE:
Berger and Luckmann's `The Social Construction of Reality` is a sociology-of-knowledge source about everyday knowledge, language, institutions, and shared reality.

INFERENCE:
Some common sense is not universal human architecture. It is produced and maintained by local practices, language, institutions, roles, socialization, and repeated interaction.

### I. AI Commonsense Reasoning

EVIDENCE:
Davis and Marcus say ordinary common sense has been a central AI challenge since the earliest days, with slow progress. They use examples involving physical objects, food, text understanding, vision, planning, and social inference.

EVIDENCE:
McCarthy's "Programs with Common Sense" framed common sense as a key AI problem, including how a machine could use declarative knowledge and choose relevant premises instead of reasoning over everything it knows.

EVIDENCE:
ConceptNet represents general knowledge as a multilingual graph. ATOMIC represents everyday if-then inferential knowledge across causes, effects, agents, mental states, and events.

EVIDENCE:
The Winograd Schema Challenge was designed to test language understanding that appears to require world knowledge. Later transformer systems scored highly, and later review work treated this as a lesson about benchmark limits and surrogate tasks rather than proof that common sense was solved.

INFERENCE:
AI research proves common sense is hard because it is broad, implicit, relevance-sensitive, defeasible, grounded in ordinary life, and not just a database of facts.

## Grouping

### Group 1 - Perceptual / Embodied

Common sense starts with body-world regularities:

- senses are integrated into a world;
- objects persist;
- bodies collide, fall, block, contain, and afford actions;
- space and time constrain movement;
- agents see, reach, intend, and fail.

### Group 2 - Cognitive / Developmental

Common sense grows through early cognitive systems:

- objects;
- number and quantity;
- places and geometry;
- agents and goals;
- social partners;
- mental states and false beliefs.

### Group 3 - Memory / Knowledge

Common sense uses organized memory:

- concepts;
- categories;
- prototypes;
- semantic networks;
- scripts;
- schemas;
- plans;
- causal patterns;
- default assumptions.

### Group 4 - Social / Cultural

Common sense is trained by shared life:

- family routines;
- community norms;
- language;
- institutions;
- roles;
- etiquette;
- law and custom;
- repeated social correction;
- what "everyone here knows."

### Group 5 - Pragmatic / Communicative

Common sense supports interaction:

- common ground;
- grounding;
- shared attention;
- turn-taking;
- implicature;
- what is relevant;
- what can be omitted;
- what must be clarified.

### Group 6 - Bounded Judgment

Common sense is the fast judgment layer:

- satisficing;
- recognition;
- rule of thumb;
- environmental fit;
- salience;
- cost of proof;
- risk threshold;
- stop condition.

### Group 7 - Defeat / Repair

Good common sense must include a defeat switch:

- this is normal unless evidence says otherwise;
- this is obvious to me but may be cultural;
- this worked before but may not fit this context;
- this is a default, not proof;
- this claim needs escalation if risk is high.

## Grouped Working Model

Common sense = ordinary-world fit judgment.

It is composed of:

1. Core perceptual integration.
2. Embodied physical affordance knowledge.
3. Early core knowledge systems.
4. Folk physics, folk biology, and folk psychology.
5. Semantic memory and concept networks.
6. Schemas, scripts, and plans.
7. Social norms and cultural doxa.
8. Common ground and pragmatic inference.
9. Bounded rationality and heuristics.
10. Feedback calibration and defeat checks.

UNKNOWN:
The literature does not support one closed inventory that is final for all cultures, domains, and future environments. The best available answer is a layered taxonomy with explicit caveats.
