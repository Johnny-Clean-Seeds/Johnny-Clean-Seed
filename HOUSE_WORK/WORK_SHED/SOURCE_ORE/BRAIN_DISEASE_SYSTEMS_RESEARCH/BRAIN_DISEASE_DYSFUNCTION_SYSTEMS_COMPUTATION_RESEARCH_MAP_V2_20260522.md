## 1. Three-lane safety model

### Lane A — Medical source lane

This lane holds what medical/neuroscience sources actually say:
- anatomy
- disease mechanisms
- symptom families
- research frameworks
- known uncertainty

Do not rewrite this lane into project doctrine.

### Lane B — Systems interpretation lane

This lane asks:
- What part of the system is failing?
- Is the failure structural, functional, metabolic, developmental, immune, vascular, electrical, timing-based, or cleanup-based?
- How does local damage spread through normal networks?
- What normal function gets distorted?

### Lane C — Clean Seed analogy lane

This lane is allowed only after source separation.

Allowed:
- use as analogy for source safety, routing, proof, indexing, alarms, loops, maintenance, and failure grammar
- build candidate rules only after specific work-use proof

Blocked:
- diagnosing the user
- self-help/medical doctrine
- treating brain disease as literal software failure
- using neurological labels as insults or social-control language
- collapsing complex disorders into one metaphor

## 2. Normal brain as layered biological computation

The brain is not one processor. It is a living multi-scale control system.

### 2.1 Substrate layer — cells, energy, vessels, chemistry

Core parts:
- neurons: signal generation and transmission
- astrocytes: metabolic support, synapse regulation, blood flow coupling, extracellular balance
- microglia: immune surveillance, pruning, inflammation, cleanup
- oligodendrocytes: myelin production and axonal support
- endothelial cells/pericytes/basement membrane: blood-brain barrier and neurovascular unit
- mitochondria: energy, calcium buffering, oxidative balance, cell-survival signaling
- lysosomes/autophagy: internal cleanup and protein/organelle recycling
- CSF/glymphatic routes: waste and solute movement
- extracellular matrix: structural and signaling environment
- immune signaling: defense, repair, inflammation, pruning pressure

Failure at this level may look psychological, cognitive, motor, or sensory because high-level function depends on low-level viability.

### 2.2 Electrical/signal layer

Core syntax:
- ion gradients
- membrane potentials
- voltage-gated and ligand-gated channels
- action potentials
- synaptic release
- excitatory/inhibitory balance
- local field rhythms
- oscillatory coordination
- neuromodulatory state

Failure patterns:
- hyperexcitability
- hypoexcitability
- poor inhibition
- slow conduction
- bad oscillatory timing
- too much synchrony
- too little synchrony
- wrong gain/precision
- unstable state switching

### 2.3 Circuit layer

Core circuit jobs:
- sensory filtering
- motor sequencing
- action selection
- memory indexing
- attention gating
- emotional salience
- language parsing/production
- prediction/error correction
- reward learning
- arousal regulation

Failure at this layer gives recognizable functions going bad: aphasia, seizures, compulsions, tics, tremor, memory loss, disinhibition, hallucination, panic, sleep instability.

### 2.4 Network layer

Large-scale networks:
- default mode network: self-model, autobiography, internal simulation
- salience network: what matters now
- executive/frontoparietal network: control, task switching, inhibition
- limbic network: threat, reward, emotion, attachment/safety
- sensorimotor network: body/movement control
- language network: sounds, meaning, grammar, production
- memory network: indexing, consolidation, retrieval
- thalamocortical network: state, relay, rhythm, attention
- cerebellar-cortical networks: timing, prediction, error correction

Modern network neuroscience often treats many disorders as dysconnectivity or disconnectivity syndromes: the problem is not only a broken node; it is the pattern of communication across nodes.

### 2.5 Algorithm-like layer

Useful computational lenses:
- prediction
- prediction error
- precision/gain weighting
- reinforcement learning
- reward prediction error
- action policy selection
- inhibitory control
- attention allocation
- uncertainty estimation
- memory pattern separation/completion
- temporal sequencing
- body-state inference
- self-agency inference

Important boundary: these are models of brain function, not proof that the brain is a digital computer.

## 3. The expanded failure grammar

Brain dysfunction can be classified by what kind of system rule is broken.

1. **Node death / degeneration**  
   Cells or tissue die or lose function.

2. **Wire damage / disconnection**  
   Axons, white matter, myelin, or long-range pathways fail.

3. **Gate failure**  
   Thalamus, basal ganglia, ion channels, inhibitory circuits, or executive control fail to open/close correctly.

4. **Timing failure**  
   Signals arrive out of phase; oscillations shift; conduction slows.

5. **Gain failure**  
   Signals are too loud, too weak, or assigned the wrong confidence.

6. **Excitation/inhibition imbalance**  
   Excitatory and inhibitory forces lose balance, causing hyperexcitability, poor filtering, or impaired computation.

7. **Synchrony failure**  
   Too much synchrony produces seizure-like flooding; too little synchrony produces poor coordination.

8. **Plasticity failure**  
   Learning and updating become too sticky, too weak, too broad, or too narrow.

9. **Prediction failure**  
   Priors, prediction errors, or uncertainty weighting are distorted.

10. **Reward/habit failure**  
    Motivation, reinforcement, habit loops, and action selection are mislearned or misgated.

11. **State failure**  
    Sleep, arousal, inflammation, stress, fuel, hormones, or autonomic state corrupt processing.

12. **Fuel failure**  
    Oxygen, glucose, mitochondrial ATP, or metabolic balance cannot support normal demand.

13. **Cleanup failure**  
    Protein folding, lysosomes, autophagy, microglial cleanup, or glymphatic waste flow breaks down.

14. **Barrier failure**  
    Blood-brain barrier / neurovascular unit loses tight regulation.

15. **Developmental construction failure**  
    Migration, pruning, myelination, synaptogenesis, or critical-period timing differs.

16. **Mass/pressure failure**  
    Tumor, edema, hydrocephalus, bleeding, or swelling compresses tissue and changes network geometry.

17. **Immune misfire**  
    Defense system attacks or inflames nervous tissue.

18. **Network-function failure without obvious structural lesion**  
    Functional neurological disorder is the clean example: real symptoms caused by altered network function rather than conventional structural damage.

19. **Hub collapse**  
    Thalamus, hippocampus, basal ganglia, brainstem, or frontal control hubs fail and disturb many downstream domains.

20. **Compensation exhaustion**  
    Normal systems compensate until reserve is exceeded; symptoms appear when redundancy fails.

## 4. “Syntax” in the brain

Syntax means ordered structure. In a brain, syntax is not only grammar. It is the ordered arrangement of signal, sequence, timing, context, and rule.

### 4.1 Ion-channel syntax

Rules:
- sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride channels open/close on schedule
- membrane thresholds decide whether a signal fires
- channel timing shapes spike trains and rhythms

Diseases:
- epilepsies
- episodic ataxias
- migraine variants
- paroxysmal dyskinesias
- some pain syndromes

Failure:
- normal-looking development may still produce sudden episodes because electrical gates are unstable.

### 4.2 Synaptic syntax

Rules:
- presynaptic release
- postsynaptic receptor response
- glutamate/GABA balance
- neuromodulator influence
- receptor trafficking
- synaptic pruning and stabilization

Diseases:
- autism-related syndromes
- schizophrenia
- epilepsy
- intellectual disability syndromes
- Alzheimer’s and other neurodegenerative disorders
- addiction and maladaptive reward learning

Failure:
- the message format is distorted; connections may exist but weights, gain, inhibition, or update rules are wrong.

### 4.3 Oscillatory syntax

Rules:
- brain regions coordinate through rhythms
- timing bands support attention, working memory, perception, movement, sleep, and consciousness
- thalamocortical rhythms help regulate state and sensory access

Diseases:
- epilepsy
- Parkinson’s disease
- tinnitus
- neuropathic pain
- depression models
- sleep disorders
- disorders of consciousness

Failure:
- the clocking layer is wrong. The system may have intact parts but bad synchronization.

### 4.4 Action syntax

Rules:
- intention → action plan → sequence → execution → feedback correction
- basal ganglia gate actions
- cerebellum calibrates error
- motor cortex executes pattern
- sensory feedback updates movement

Diseases:
- Parkinson’s disease
- Huntington’s disease
- dystonia
- Tourette syndrome/tics
- apraxia
- cerebellar ataxia
- stroke
- ALS / motor neuron disease

Failure:
- the body may know what it wants, but start/stop/sequence/calibration paths fail.

### 4.5 Language syntax

Rules:
- sound parsing
- word access
- grammar/sequencing
- semantic mapping
- speech motor production
- comprehension
- pragmatic/social meaning

Diseases:
- aphasia after stroke
- primary progressive aphasia
- dementia
- TBI
- psychosis/disorganized speech
- developmental language disorder
- motor speech disorders

Failure:
- syntax can fail at sound, word, grammar, meaning, speech planning, or social-use level.

### 4.6 Thought syntax

Rules:
- working memory keeps pieces online
- executive systems order goals
- salience decides what matters
- predictive processing interprets evidence
- inhibition blocks irrelevant branches

Diseases:
- schizophrenia/psychosis
- mania
- depression
- anxiety/PTSD
- ADHD
- dementia
- delirium
- TBI
- sleep deprivation

Failure:
- thought may become too loose, too rigid, too threat-weighted, too fast, too slow, or poorly gated.

### 4.7 Social syntax

Rules:
- self/other separation
- agency inference
- status/threat detection
- empathy/mentalizing
- social rule memory
- inhibition and context

Diseases/dysfunctions:
- frontotemporal dementia
- autism spectrum conditions
- psychosis
- TBI
- orbitofrontal damage
- some personality/impulse regulation disorders

Failure:
- social meaning is misread, missing, over-weighted, under-inhibited, or no longer governed by learned rules.

## 5. Disease and dysfunction families — expanded systems view

### 5.1 Neurodegeneration / proteinopathy / progressive network loss

Examples:
- Alzheimer’s disease
- Parkinson’s disease
- Huntington’s disease
- Lewy body dementia
- frontotemporal dementia
- ALS / motor neuron disease
- spinocerebellar ataxias
- prion diseases

Common mechanisms:
- abnormal protein folding/aggregation
- synaptic failure
- mitochondrial stress
- neuroinflammation
- axonal transport disruption
- selective cell vulnerability
- lysosome/autophagy impairment
- progressive network disconnection

Normal parts affected:
- Alzheimer’s: memory/indexing systems early; later cortex/network cognition
- Parkinson’s: basal ganglia action gate; later cognition, mood, autonomic/sleep systems
- Huntington’s: basal ganglia/cortex; action release, cognition, mood
- FTD: frontal/temporal social-behavior-language systems
- ALS: motor neurons and motor networks; later cognitive/behavioral overlap in some cases
- ataxias: cerebellar calibration systems

Computational effect:
- memory index loss
- semantic degradation
- action policy failure
- calibration failure
- motor command loss
- social rule collapse
- hub degradation and compensation exhaustion

Clean Seed analogy:
- not just “one file corrupted”; the maintenance, index, action gate, and cleanup systems progressively lose reliability.

### 5.2 Vascular / neurovascular / blood-brain barrier failure

Examples:
- ischemic stroke
- hemorrhagic stroke
- small-vessel disease
- vascular cognitive impairment
- CADASIL and other inherited vascular disorders
- BBB dysfunction in neuroinflammation/neurodegeneration

Core mechanism:
- oxygen/glucose delivery is blocked, bleeding damages tissue, or vessel regulation fails
- BBB/NVU dysfunction can let inflammatory or toxic signals affect the brain environment
- small-vessel disease causes cumulative white-matter and executive-routing decline

Normal parts affected:
- motor pathways
- language areas
- visual fields
- memory circuits
- frontal-subcortical executive loops
- white matter
- brainstem/cerebellar circulation

Computational effect:
- power/fuel loss
- local module offline
- route cut
- hub failure
- degraded executive speed
- impaired network homeostasis

Clean Seed analogy:
- a workroom may be intact, but the power/data/fuel supply is cut or contaminated.

### 5.3 Demyelination / white-matter / wiring insulation failure

Examples:
- multiple sclerosis
- neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder
- MOG antibody disease
- leukodystrophies
- vascular white matter disease
- diffuse axonal injury after TBI

Core mechanism:
- myelin/axons fail
- signal conduction slows, blocks, or desynchronizes
- long-range communication loses timing

Normal parts affected:
- optic nerves
- spinal cord
- cerebellar pathways
- sensory/motor tracts
- cognitive networks

Computational effect:
- latency increase
- dropped packets
- intermittent errors
- distributed coordination failure
- fatigue under load

Clean Seed analogy:
- good modules fail because messages arrive late, weak, or not at all.

### 5.4 Epilepsy / hyperexcitability / synchrony failure

Examples:
- focal epilepsy
- generalized epilepsy
- temporal-lobe epilepsy
- developmental epileptic encephalopathies
- post-stroke or post-TBI seizures

Core mechanism:
- abnormal excessive synchronous neural activity
- poor inhibition or hyperexcitable circuits
- local seizure focus can recruit larger networks

Normal parts affected:
- motor output
- memory/awareness
- sensory perception
- language
- behavior/emotion
- sleep

Computational effect:
- information specificity is drowned by synchronized activity
- local storm overrides normal signal routing
- postictal state creates temporary low-function mode

Clean Seed analogy:
- all alarms fire together; the system cannot distinguish the actual signal.

### 5.5 Channelopathies / ion-gate disorders

Examples:
- inherited epilepsies
- episodic ataxia
- familial hemiplegic migraine
- some periodic paralysis/paroxysmal dyskinesia syndromes
- pain disorders involving channel dysfunction

Core mechanism:
- ion channels open/close incorrectly
- excitability thresholds shift
- episodic dysfunction appears against otherwise normal background

Normal parts affected:
- motor control
- sensory pain systems
- cerebellar timing
- cortical excitability
- autonomic/sensory episodes

Computational effect:
- the gate threshold is wrong
- function may be normal until a trigger pushes the system into a bad state

Clean Seed analogy:
- a switch is fine at rest but fails under certain load/trigger conditions.

### 5.6 Synaptopathies / receptor / neurotransmitter system dysfunction

Examples:
- autism-related syndromes
- schizophrenia
- epilepsy
- intellectual disability syndromes
- Alzheimer’s
- addiction
- ADHD
- mood disorders
- Parkinson’s dopamine-system disruption

Core mechanism:
- synaptic release, receptors, scaffolding proteins, pruning, receptor trafficking, or neuromodulator balance is altered
- glutamate/GABA excitation-inhibition balance may shift
- dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, acetylcholine, GABA, glutamate systems influence large domains of mood, motivation, attention, memory, movement, and arousal

Normal parts affected:
- learning
- attention
- reward
- memory
- inhibitory control
- sensory filtering
- social processing
- movement selection

Computational effect:
- bad weights
- bad update rules
- bad confidence/gain
- bad reward prediction
- poor filtering

Clean Seed analogy:
- the link exists, but the weight/reward/gate attached to it is wrong.

### 5.7 Glial dysfunction / support-system failure

Examples/links:
- MS/oligodendrocyte-myelin disease
- neuroinflammatory disorders
- neurodegeneration
- autism/schizophrenia research links
- chronic pain states
- TBI inflammation and repair

Core mechanism:
- astrocytes fail metabolic/synaptic/vascular support
- microglia over-prune, under-clear, or inflame
- oligodendrocytes fail myelin/axon support
- glia-neuron signaling becomes maladaptive

Normal parts affected:
- synapse stability
- myelin integrity
- energy balance
- blood flow matching
- immune cleanup
- extracellular ion/neurotransmitter balance

Computational effect:
- support layer corrupts signal quality
- cleanup becomes damage
- repair becomes inflammation
- wiring and energy support decline

Clean Seed analogy:
- maintenance crew, insulation crew, cleanup crew, and environment monitors are part of computation; if support is sick, core modules malfunction.

### 5.8 Mitochondrial / metabolic / fuel-state dysfunction

Examples:
- mitochondrial disease
- hypoxia/ischemia
- hypoglycemia
- liver/kidney encephalopathy
- thyroid/endocrine effects
- B12 deficiency
- medication/toxin effects
- neurodegenerative disease mitochondrial stress
- stroke and TBI metabolic crisis

Core mechanism:
- brain energy supply/demand mismatch
- mitochondria cannot maintain ATP/calcium/redox balance
- systemic chemistry disrupts neural function

Normal parts affected:
- high-demand cortex
- basal ganglia
- cerebellum
- optic/retinal pathways
- motor neurons
- developing brain

Computational effect:
- low-power mode
- slowed processing
- poor recovery after firing
- vulnerability to overload
- global state corruption

Clean Seed analogy:
- good code under bad voltage/fuel still behaves badly.

### 5.9 Autophagy / lysosome / protein cleanup failure

Examples:
- Alzheimer’s
- Parkinson’s
- FTD
- ALS
- lysosomal storage diseases
- some genetic neurodegenerative syndromes

Core mechanism:
- cells fail to clear misfolded proteins, damaged organelles, or waste
- aggregates, trafficking failures, and impaired recycling injure synapses and neurons

Normal parts affected:
- synapses
- axons
- cell bodies
- glial cells
- long-lived high-demand neurons

Computational effect:
- garbage accumulation
- repair queue overload
- progressive slow corruption
- cell stress and death

Clean Seed analogy:
- if cleanup and recycling fail, the system rots even if the active task looked fine.

### 5.10 Glymphatic / CSF / sleep-linked waste and state system

Examples/links:
- Alzheimer’s and dementia research
- sleep deprivation
- TBI
- stroke
- epilepsy
- aging-related clearance questions

Core mechanism:
- CSF/interstitial fluid movement clears solutes and waste

Normal parts affected:
- protein clearance
- inflammation
- vascular pulsatility/CSF dynamics
- sleep architecture
- long-term cognitive health

Computational effect:
- bad overnight cleanup
- accumulated noise/waste
- poorer memory/consolidation/state reset

Clean Seed analogy:
- the nightly cleanup/checkpoint lane matters; without it, old junk and bad state carry into new work.

## 7. Clean Seed “brain failure grammar” for project use

This section is analogy only.

### 7.1 Source-safe import rule

Never import a medical concept straight into the model. Use this order:

1. Source fact.
2. Systems mechanism.
3. Clean Seed analogy.
4. Candidate rule.
5. Live-use proof.
6. Promotion decision.

### 7.2 Project failure correspondences

| Brain failure | Project analogy | Clean use |
|---|---|---|
| Node death | missing/broken artifact | verify file/module exists and works |
| Wire damage | broken path/link | check route, pointers, handoff lane |
| Myelin failure | slow/noisy route | insulate commands, reduce copy/paste corruption |
| Gate failure | wrong next action allowed | strengthen authority and selection gate |
| Seizure/synchrony | all alarms fire | reduce simultaneous triggers |
| E/I imbalance | too much activation or suppression | tune alarms vs movement |
| Bad gain | weak signal overweighted or strong signal ignored | define severity weights |
| Prediction error | false assumption drives route | require proof/reality check |
| Reward loop | tool use feels productive but does not move object | check object movement |
| Cleanup failure | old junk corrupts work | clean indexes, receipts, parking lanes |
| Glymphatic failure | bad state carries over | run house walk / re-entry cleanup |
| Vascular failure | resource/fuel lane blocked | check access, paths, compute, source availability |
| FND-like network failure | structure looks okay but execution fails | test actual behavior, not just file existence |
| Developmental issue | old construction decision shapes current bug | inspect history before judging |
| Compensation exhaustion | system held until load exceeded | reduce load and repair root support |

### 7.3 Clean Seed alarms this research supports

Use this map when:
- everything fires at once
- a command works but the route is wrong
- a file exists but authority is unclear
- source and reframe mix
- a tool becomes the reward
- proof exists but does not prove the current target
- state/fog is carried into the next task
- the system overcorrects after one failure
- repeated loop failure looks like a “personality” problem but is actually route/gate/state failure
- a local support lane is sick but active artifacts look normal

## 8. Review / revise pass

### 8.1 What v1 got right

- It framed brain diseases as systems failures, not isolated symptom labels.
- It separated substrate, circuits, networks, and computation-like functions.
- It included a useful “failure grammar.”
- It protected Clean Seed from direct medical-doctrine import.

### 8.2 What v1 missed or underweighted

- Glia are not optional support; they are active computation/support/cleanup/immune participants.
- Blood-brain barrier and neurovascular unit failures can corrupt the brain environment without a classic “brain part” lesion.
- Cleanup systems matter: lysosome/autophagy/glymphatic failure can produce slow rot, not sudden visible break.
- Channelopathies and oscillation disorders show that syntax can fail at the gate/timing level while anatomy appears mostly intact.
- FND is important because it demonstrates real dysfunction can be network-functional without ordinary structural lesion.
- Psychiatric disorders need multi-level framing, not “chemical imbalance only” or “psychological only.”
- Sleep is not just rest; it is state regulation, memory, arousal, and likely clearance-related maintenance.

## 9. Read ladder

### Ladder 1 — Normal brain architecture

Read:
- NINDS Brain Basics: Know Your Brain.
- NCBI/StatPearls neurotransmitters overview.
- Standard anatomy sources for cortex, thalamus, basal ganglia, cerebellum, hippocampus, limbic system, brainstem.

Goal:
Understand normal rooms before describing broken rooms.

### Ladder 2 — Infrastructure layers

Read:
- neurovascular unit / blood-brain barrier
- glial function
- mitochondrial brain disease
- autophagy/lysosome dysfunction
- glymphatic system and sleep-linked clearance
- white matter/myelin disease

Goal:
Stop treating neurons alone as the whole brain.

### Ladder 3 — Electrical/syntax layers

Read:
- epilepsy / hyperexcitability
- channelopathies
- excitation/inhibition balance
- thalamocortical dysrhythmia
- migraine / cortical spreading depression

Goal:
Understand gates, clocks, gain, and synchronization.

### Ladder 4 — Network/computation layers

Read:
- connectome/dysconnectivity
- computational psychiatry
- RDoC
- predictive processing
- reinforcement learning / reward prediction
- salience network and executive control

Goal:
Understand why symptoms can emerge from network rules, not just dead tissue.

### Ladder 5 — Clean Seed reframe

Steps:
1. Keep source safe.
2. Identify actual mechanism.
3. Translate only after mechanism is understood.
4. Mark candidate status.
5. Test against a real project failure.
6. Promote only with proof.
