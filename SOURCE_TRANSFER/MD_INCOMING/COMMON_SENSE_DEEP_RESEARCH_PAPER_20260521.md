# 1. Executive Thesis

Common sense is the ordinary living ability to perceive a situation, recognize what matters, use shared background knowledge, judge what is reasonable under the circumstances, and act proportionately without needing a full explicit theory.

A stronger definition:

**Common sense is embodied, socially shaped, tacit, situation-sensitive practical judgment that lets a person or community move through ordinary reality by recognizing what is probably true, what matters now, what actions are available, what others can be expected to understand, and what response is proportionate under the circumstances.**

It is not merely "obvious truth."

It is not merely instinct.

It is not merely cultural convention.

It is not merely logic.

It is not merely experience.

It is a working blend of:

- perception;
- memory;
- pattern recognition;
- practical reason;
- social norms;
- tacit skill;
- environmental affordances;
- language/common ground;
- proportion;
- timing;
- consequence;
- humility before feedback.

Common sense is powerful because it compresses reality into action.

Common sense is dangerous because the compression can be wrong, stale, biased, culture-bound, overconfident, or misapplied outside its original environment.

# 2. The Core Layers Of Common Sense

## 2.1 Perceptual integration

The older philosophical root of "common sense" is not "good judgment" but a faculty that unifies sensory inputs into a coherent perceived world. In Aristotelian tradition, the sensus communis is where different senses are coordinated so a living creature does not merely see color, hear sound, and feel texture separately, but perceives things as unified objects with motion, shape, number, and change.

This matters because common sense starts before argument. It begins in the ability to experience a situation as a situation.

House translation:

```text
raw signals
-> unified scene
-> object/actor/action recognition
-> what matters
-> possible response
```

If perceptual integration fails, judgment starts on a broken world model.

## 2.2 Practical judgment

Practical reason is concerned with what one is to do. The important thing here is that common sense is action-facing. It is not only a belief about what is true; it is a judgment about what to do next.

Common sense asks:

```text
What is happening?
What matters?
What can be done?
What should not be done?
What is enough?
What is too much?
What is safe?
What is fair?
What is likely?
What is the next reasonable move?
```

Practical reason gives common sense its action-direction.

## 2.3 Practical wisdom

Aristotle's practical wisdom, phronesis, is close to the mature form of common sense. It cannot be reduced to general rules. A person must learn how to see the relevant particulars in a situation and apply judgment with emotional, social, and practical competence.

This is a key point:

**Common sense cannot be fully replaced by a rulebook.**

Rules help. But common sense is the ability to know which rule matters, when it matters, how far it reaches, and when a different feature of the situation is more important.

House translation:

```text
rule exists
-> situation appears
-> relevant particulars noticed
-> rule fit judged
-> exception/boundary considered
-> action chosen
-> feedback absorbed
```

## 2.4 Shared background knowledge

Common sense is also common. It depends on what a group treats as obvious, ordinary, or taken for granted.

This is useful because it lets people coordinate without explaining every premise.

Example:

```text
If someone says "Watch out, the floor is wet,"
they do not need to explain gravity, slipping, bodily injury, cleaning, or walking.
The shared background does most of the work.
```

But this is also risky. What a group treats as "obvious" can include prejudice, local habits, bad assumptions, outdated facts, or unexamined power.

## 2.5 Common ground in language

In pragmatics, common ground is the information presumed shared by speakers. Communication works because people do not only decode words. They infer meaning from context, norms, background knowledge, expectations, and the cooperative shape of conversation.

Common sense therefore has a linguistic dimension:

```text
what was said
+ what was implied
+ who said it
+ where/when it was said
+ what both parties likely know
+ what response fits
```

A person with good common sense reads the room. That includes tone, timing, implied request, urgency, and what does not need to be said aloud.

## 2.6 Tacit knowledge

Much common sense is tacit. People often know how to do something without being able to fully explain how they know.

Examples:

- knowing how close to stand in conversation;
- sensing that a tool is about to slip;
- knowing a joke will not land in a room;
- noticing that a procedure is formally correct but practically wrong;
- knowing a local shortcut;
- feeling that a plan is overbuilt.

Tacit knowledge is not mystical. It is accumulated embodied pattern recognition, skill, and context awareness.

## 2.7 Habitus and practical sense

Bourdieu's habitus/practical sense points to the socially formed "feel for the game." People move through social fields by internalizing patterns of what fits, what counts, what signals status, what is normal, what is risky, and what is possible.

This helps explain why common sense varies across groups and contexts. The common sense of a machine shop, courtroom, family kitchen, church, farm, school, prison, hospital, subreddit, or software project is not identical.

Common sense is partly local field literacy.

## 2.8 Affordance perception

Gibson's affordance idea is central. Common sense includes perceiving what the environment offers for action.

A chair affords sitting. A handle affords pulling. A narrow icy path affords caution. A messy desk affords searching but not quick retrieval. A bright red warning label affords stopping.

Common sense is not just "knowing facts." It is seeing action-possibilities in context.

House translation:

```text
room
-> objects
-> affordances
-> risks
-> possible moves
-> clean next action
```

## 2.9 Bounded rationality and heuristics

Humans usually do not have unlimited time, information, or calculation. Common sense works under bounded conditions.

It often uses heuristics:

- if unsure, slow down;
- do not touch a hot stove;
- check the obvious first;
- if the floor is wet, walk carefully;
- if a tool failed once, inspect before reuse;
- if a return folder is dirty, do not commit unrelated work over it.

Heuristics are not automatically bad. Under the right environmental structure, fast and frugal heuristics can be effective. But heuristics can also create systematic bias.

So common sense is not anti-rational. It is bounded practical rationality.

## 2.10 Reasonableness

Law gives a useful public form of common sense: the reasonable person. The point is not perfect wisdom. It is a standard of prudent action under circumstances.

This gives common sense a public-test shape:

```text
Would a reasonably careful person, knowing what was available in this situation, act this way?
Was the action proportionate?
Was the risk foreseeable?
Was the precaution reasonable?
```

This is useful because common sense is not just private feeling. It can be judged publicly against circumstances.

# 3. What Common Sense Is Not

## 3.1 Not mere instinct

Instinct is automatic tendency. Common sense includes learned pattern, social knowledge, norm awareness, and consequence judgment.

## 3.2 Not mere intelligence

A person can be highly analytical and lack common sense. They may know abstract rules but miss practical fit.

## 3.3 Not mere tradition

Tradition can carry common sense, but it can also carry stale or harmful assumptions.

## 3.4 Not always correct

"Common sense" once supported many wrong beliefs. The fact that something feels obvious does not prove it is true.

## 3.5 Not culture-free

Different groups have different taken-for-granted knowledge. Common sense is partly cultural.

## 3.6 Not a substitute for evidence

Common sense helps decide where to look, how to act, and when something feels wrong. Evidence checks whether the feeling survives contact with reality.

## 3.7 Not endless skepticism

Common sense also prevents paralysis. It says: enough is known for the next reasonable step.

# 4. The Fundamental Structure

The deepest structure of common sense can be modeled as a loop:

```text
SITUATION CONTACT
-> PATTERN RECOGNITION
-> COMMON GROUND
-> AFFORDANCE DETECTION
-> PRACTICAL REASON
-> PROPORTION CHECK
-> SOCIAL/ETHICAL FIT
-> ACTION
-> FEEDBACK
-> CALIBRATION
```

Expanded:

## 4.1 Situation contact

Common sense begins by touching the actual situation, not a fantasy version of it.

Questions:

```text
What is actually here?
What changed?
What is dirty/clean?
What is active/stale?
What is proven/claimed?
What is risky?
What is asking for attention?
```

## 4.2 Pattern recognition

The mind compares the present case to past cases.

Questions:

```text
Have I seen this before?
What does this resemble?
What usually happens next?
What hidden danger may be present?
```

## 4.3 Common ground

Common sense checks what is shared or assumed.

Questions:

```text
What do we both know?
What does the room expect?
What is the local rule?
What does not need to be explained?
What does need to be clarified?
```

## 4.4 Affordance detection

Common sense sees possible moves.

Questions:

```text
What can be done now?
What tool fits?
What path is open?
What path is blocked?
What would be overreach?
```

## 4.5 Practical reason

Common sense chooses action.

Questions:

```text
What should be done?
What should wait?
What is the smallest clean move?
What must not be touched?
What proof is needed?
```

## 4.6 Proportion check

Common sense avoids both underreaction and overreaction.

Questions:

```text
Is this enough?
Is this too much?
Is this premature?
Is this too late?
Is this reversible?
```

## 4.7 Social/ethical fit

Common sense accounts for people and norms.

Questions:

```text
Will this make sense to others?
Is this fair?
Does this respect role/boundary?
Does this create unnecessary burden?
```

## 4.8 Feedback and calibration

Common sense learns.

Questions:

```text
Did the action work?
What did it miss?
What changed?
Should the rule strengthen, weaken, decay, or split?
```

# 6. Bad Common Sense

Common sense fails in recurring ways.

## 6.1 Local obviousness mistaken for universal truth

A belief feels obvious because everyone nearby shares it. That does not make it universal.

## 6.2 Stale common sense

A rule worked in an old environment and fails in a new one.

## 6.3 Overgeneralized pattern

A pattern from one case is applied to a different case.

## 6.4 Cultural blind spot

A group's common sense hides another group's reality.

## 6.5 Bias disguised as obviousness

Prejudice often presents itself as "just common sense."

## 6.6 Speed without calibration

Fast judgment becomes reckless when stakes are high or signals are novel.

## 6.7 Rule without particulars

A rule is applied mechanically without seeing the situation.

## 6.8 Particulars without rule

A person reacts to details without any stabilizing principle.

## 6.9 Practical cleverness without ethics

A move "works" but is unfair, unsafe, or corrupting.

## 6.10 Evidence refusal

The person treats their sense of obviousness as stronger than proof.

# 7. Good Common Sense

Good common sense has these features.

## 7.1 Contact with reality

It starts from what is actually happening.

## 7.2 Situation fit

It knows that right action depends on circumstance.

## 7.3 Practical movement

It leads to action, not endless abstraction.

## 7.4 Proportion

It does not overbuild or underreact.

## 7.5 Social legibility

Other competent participants can understand why the move made sense.

## 7.6 Boundary respect

It knows what not to touch.

## 7.7 Reversibility

When uncertain, it prefers small reversible moves.

## 7.8 Feedback learning

It changes when reality pushes back.

## 7.9 Humility

It does not confuse familiar with true.

## 7.10 Care

It accounts for harm, fairness, and responsibility.

# 8. The Relationship Between Common Sense And Rules

Common sense and rules need each other.

Rules without common sense become brittle.

Common sense without rules becomes vague, biased, or inconsistent.

Healthy relationship:

```text
rule gives stability
common sense gives fit
proof gives correction
feedback gives learning
```

A rule says:

```text
Do not save over dirty repo state.
```

Common sense sees:

```text
The dirty state is a mule return, so this is not just dirt. It is active guest output waiting for intake.
```

A rule says:

```text
Tools need passports.
```

Common sense sees:

```text
This bridge belongs in Mule Workshop because that is where assistant and mule exchange files; 123 is only porch/storage.
```

A rule says:

```text
Do not overbuild.
```

Common sense sees:

```text
A bridge is not burden if it solves the real visibility/file-exchange problem and has rooms, boundaries, and proof.
```

This is the key:

**Common sense is rule-fit judgment under real conditions.**

# 9. The Relationship Between Common Sense And Proof

Common sense is not proof, but it guides proof.

It helps decide:

```text
What needs proof?
What kind of proof fits?
When is proof enough?
What proof can be gamed?
What proof is stale?
What should be checked first?
```

Common sense says:

```text
Do not judge a mule return from file names alone.
```

Proof says:

```text
Read the files, hash them, preserve custody, and issue a content verdict.
```

Common sense says:

```text
Do not run the bridge save over a dirty return.
```

Proof says:

```text
git status shows the dirty return folder.
```

Common sense and proof are partners:

```text
common sense chooses the proof route
proof corrects common sense
```

# 10. The Relationship Between Common Sense And Research

Research can strengthen common sense by revealing hidden structure.

But research can also bury common sense in complexity.

Good research does three things:

```text
names what was felt;
shows what was missing;
gives a smaller cleaner test.
```

Bad research does three things:

```text
adds terms without action;
turns source authority into command;
creates a pile instead of a route.
```

For Mr.Kleen, common-sense research should become:

```text
comparison
-> ability capture
-> proof route
-> state label
-> link map
-> save/lock or park
```

# 13. Rule Draft: Common Sense Gate

```text
# Common Sense Gate

When an action is technically possible but may be practically wrong, the assistant must fire the Common Sense Gate before moving.

The assistant shall check ground, room, role, authority, fit, proportion, consequence, reversibility, proof, common ground, boundary, and quiet.

The gate outputs one of: move now, wait, read first, ask/clarify, park, block, save/lock, tiny test, route to mule, route to house, route to 123, archive/decay.

This gate does not override proof. It chooses the proof route.

This gate does not override authority. It checks authority.

This gate does not replace rules. It judges rule fit under the current situation.

If the assistant acts against obvious situation-fit and the user has to correct placement, scope, timing, or practicality, treat that as Common Sense Gate late-fire or dead-nerve failure.
```

# 17. What Common Sense Fundamentally Is

Final formulation:

**Common sense is the living fit-sense by which embodied, social, bounded agents convert ordinary contact with reality into proportionate action under uncertainty.**

It has five foundations:

1. **World contact**: seeing the situation.
2. **Shared background**: knowing what is commonly assumed.
3. **Practical judgment**: knowing what to do.
4. **Tacit fit**: sensing what belongs without always articulating why.
5. **Feedback correction**: changing when reality proves the first sense wrong.

It has five dangers:

1. **staleness**;
2. **bias**;
3. **overconfidence**;
4. **localness mistaken for universality**;
5. **resistance to evidence**.

It has five clean outputs:

1. **reasonable next move**;
2. **proper room/tool/action fit**;
3. **proportionate response**;
4. **proof route**;
5. **quiet or movement at the right time**.

# 19. Final Clean Sentence

**Common sense is not a pile of obvious beliefs. It is the living ability to stay in contact with the situation, see what matters, choose a proportionate next move, respect shared reality and boundary, and let feedback correct what once felt obvious.**
